Paper 02 · Dissertation Deep Dive · Study 2

The equity power of high-capacity schools.

Organizational capacity doesn't benefit all students equally — and that's the point. The students facing the greatest institutional barriers gain the most from high-capacity schools. This study asks for whom capacity matters, in which contexts, and across which timescales.

Why equity, why now

The pandemic exposed how unevenly schools absorb disruption: students in low-income neighborhoods suffered lasting learning losses while advantaged peers were largely unharmed, and the same crisis destabilized experienced staffing and stable leadership precisely where they were needed most. Schools are not neutral sites of instruction — they are institutional environments that amplify or mitigate inequity through their capacity configurations.

Research questions

RQ1 · Microsystem

Is organizational capacity, as measured by the CCI, associated with achievement across different student populations?

RQ2 · Chronosystem

Is campus capacity associated with long-term success metrics — four-year and two-year college enrollment?

RQ3 · Mesosystem

Do these relationships vary across school contexts — urbanicity, governance, and regional higher-education ecosystems?

Theoretical move: an ecological lens on the CCI

This study extends organizational capacity theory through three of Bronfenbrenner's ecological systems. Rather than assuming capacity operates uniformly, its effects emerge from the nested environments students live in — and each ring maps to a research question.

  • Microsystem → RQ1: classroom processes — experienced teachers and stable leadership alter how students engage with instruction, with the largest gains for students needing specialized support
  • Mesosystem → RQ3: community & institutional context — urbanicity, governance, and regional resources amplify or constrain capacity's reach
  • Chronosystem → RQ2: time & transitions — capacity shapes aspirations and college pathways over years, not just test scores

The theory guides where to look — the study investigates the CCI's equity mechanisms, not the theory itself.

Data & methods

Two complementary samples — one for achievement across all campuses, one for college pathways among high schools — analyzed with the same staged fixed-effects design.

7,983High-school-year observations for college enrollment (1,465 high schools)

25.15%Average two-year college enrollment rate

📊 Data sources

  • TAPR — the CCI's indicators plus achievement disaggregated by seven subgroups: economically disadvantaged, African American, Hispanic, emergent bilingual, special education, at-risk, and female students
  • AskTED — urbanicity (NCES locale codes collapsed to City / Suburban / Town / Rural), charter status, ESC regions
  • THECB — campus-level two- and four-year public college enrollment (Texas public institutions; private and out-of-state not captured)
  • University Hubs — eight regional higher-education ecosystems built by aggregating ESC regions (DFW Metro, Central TX, Houston Metro, San Antonio, South TX, West TX, East TX, Other/Rural)

🧭 Analytical approach

Year FE: Yit = β0 + β1CCIit + β2Xit + δt + εit
District FE: Yit = β0 + β1CCIit + β2Xit + δt + δd + εit

Year-only models capture the total statewide association (upper bound); district fixed effects compare campuses within the same district (conservative). Controls: standardized economic disadvantage, plus enrollment counts for college models. SEs clustered by district. RQ3 adds stratified models and formal interaction tests.

Finding 1 — Capacity is an equity mechanism

Capacity advantages are significantly larger for historically marginalized populations — the students who require the most institutional support gain the most from high-capacity schools.

Optimal vs. Minimal Capacity, district + year FE (conservative within-district estimates). Year-only estimates run roughly 1–3pp higher — e.g., 8.33pp math for emergent bilingual students.

Largest gains
Emergent bilingual students

The strongest capacity benefits in the study: +6.27pp math and +5.96pp reading within districts. Even Moderate Capacity confers significant advantages (2.11–3.51pp) — consistent with the theory that EB students benefit most from strategically deployed instructional expertise.

Specialized support
Students with disabilities

+3.32pp math and +3.52pp reading with district controls (year-only models reach 5.33pp) — evidence that stability and experienced staff foster the conditions for inclusive, specialized instruction.

Subject pattern
Math > reading, everywhere

Mathematics associations are stronger across every population, suggesting math instruction depends more on systematic organizational approaches, while reading is shaped by broader factors like home environments the CCI can't reach.

The equity interpretation

The differential benefits reveal that the CCI operates as an equity-enhancing mechanism rather than amplifying existing advantage: it reflects schools' ability to build and sustain the specialized systems that students facing the greatest institutional barriers need most. This shifts the frame from deficit-based explanations of achievement gaps toward institutional solutions.

Finding 2 — Capacity channels students toward four-year colleges

High-capacity high schools don't just raise college-going — they shape which college pathway students take, boosting four-year enrollment while systematically reducing two-year enrollment.

Optimal vs. Minimal Capacity effects on college enrollment (pp), district + year FE. Within districts, even Moderate Capacity confers a significant +1.04pp four-year advantage.

Governance
The charter reveal

Across districts, charter capacity looks ineffective for college-going (non-significant estimates). But within districts, Optimal Capacity charters show a +5.24pp four-year advantage — among the largest in the study — alongside the strongest channeling away from two-year colleges (−3.51pp). Charter success appears highly dependent on district-level regulatory support and higher-ed relationships.

Geography
Rural schools don't channel

Non-rural Optimal schools show large four-year advantages (+6.16pp within districts) with clear channeling (−1.41pp two-year). Rural schools show only modest four-year gains (+2.55pp, year-only) and slightly positive two-year effects — community colleges remain a vital pathway where four-year access is constrained.

The channeling phenomenon

The consistent negative two-year effects across most contexts suggest high-capacity schools develop institutional cultures, counseling systems, and academic programs that orient students toward four-year institutions over extended periods — a chronosystem effect on developmental trajectories, not just a bump in achievement. It also raises genuine equity questions about pathway diversity and the role of community colleges in rural economies.

Finding 3 — Context shapes how far capacity reaches

The same organizational capacity produces different returns depending on the resource environment around the school — the mesosystem at work.

Optimal vs. Minimal achievement advantages (pp) by urbanicity, district + year FE.

Optimal Capacity four-year enrollment advantages (pp) by university hub, district FE. Channeling from two-year colleges is significant only in the big metros (Houston −1.88, DFW −0.98, San Antonio −1.79).

Standout
Non-rural charters lead achievement

Optimal Capacity non-rural charters post the largest achievement estimates in any context: +7.90pp math and +7.91pp reading within districts — while rural charters show non-significant negatives, likely reflecting tiny samples.

Anomaly
The Central Texas puzzle

The Austin–Waco hub shows negative and null four-year estimates — possibly reflecting high private-college attendance the data can't see, or the competitive admissions market around the state's flagships. A genuinely interesting open question.

Honest caveat
Interaction tests: not significant

Stratified analyses show meaningful practical differences, but formal moderation tests are non-significant (only rural × reading approaches the 0.10 threshold at p = 0.095). Contextual variation is real in pattern but operates through complex mechanisms formal tests don't cleanly capture.

A surprise worth noting

The region with the highest proportion of Optimal Capacity campuses isn't a metro at all — it's Edinburg (Region 1) at 31.2%, on the state's southern border. And the Other/Rural hub shows a relatively high Optimal share (17.4%), evidence that some rural schools build strong organizational capacity despite labor-market constraints.

Takeaways

What Paper 2 establishes — and the question it hands to Paper 3.

Equity
Capacity compensates for systemic disadvantage

The largest benefits accrue to emergent bilingual students and students with disabilities — a measurable, institutional lever for educational equity rather than a deficit-based account of gaps.

Context
Effectiveness is context-dependent

From +7.90pp in non-rural charters to modest rural estimates, environmental resources fundamentally shape capacity returns — school effectiveness should be valued within local contexts rather than through uniform statewide metrics.

Trajectories
Capacity shapes pathways, not just scores

The channeling effect shows organizational capacity operating on students' long-run trajectories — influencing aspirations and transitions over years, exactly as the chronosystem predicts.

Limitations

The design is correlational — it strengthens the CCI's measurement framework and motivates causal work, but cannot establish causality. THECB enrollment data cover Texas public institutions only, missing private and out-of-state pathways. And subgroup analyses use separate models by necessity of the administrative data structure, preventing direct cross-subgroup comparisons within a single model.

The question this paper hands to Paper 3

If organizational capacity can compensate for systemic disadvantage and deliver the greatest benefits to the students who need institutional support most, the critical question becomes: which policies can reliably build that capacity?
