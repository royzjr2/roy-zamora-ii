📄 Working paper — 2026 draft

From incentives to capacity: how the Texas Teacher Incentive Allotment reshapes schools.

Most merit pay tries to buy individual performance. TIA requires districts to build organizational infrastructure first. Using staggered difference-in-differences across 56,939 campus-year observations (2017–2024), this study finds the policy works — but through capacity development, not direct incentives.

The core finding — TIA's effects travel through organizational capacity

Buying performance vs. building capacity

Why the Teacher Incentive Allotment is a different kind of performance-based compensation — and why that difference is the study's central question.

The old model

Traditional merit pay: "buying" teacher quality

Individual financial incentives overlaid on existing structures, assuming clear performance signals and rational-actor responses. In practice, education is a sea of "imperfect signals" — noisy effectiveness measures shaped by prior achievement, socioeconomics, and resources. Large RCTs of traditional merit pay have shown minimal effects, alongside short-term thinking and unsustainable resource dependence.

TIA's innovation

TIA (2019): capacity first, compensation second

Districts must build organizational infrastructure — calibrated evaluation systems, data management capabilities, stakeholder engagement — before participating. Once approved, state funding flows per designated highly-effective teacher. The approval process itself creates new "rules-in-use" that reshape how districts approach evaluation and strategic staffing.

Theoretical framework: IAD × agency theory

The Institutional Analysis & Development framework treats TIA as reshaping the decision-making environment — new rules-in-use rather than a simple bonus scheme. Agency theory explains the cascading principal–agent chain: the state is principal to districts, districts to campuses, campus administrators to teachers. TIA's capacity prerequisites address the information asymmetries that break traditional merit pay at each link in that chain.

Research questions

RQ1

Does TIA approval lead to changes in the CCI — and do effects vary across its capacity dimensions?

RQ2

Do TIA's effects on the CCI vary across school contexts — rural, high-poverty, and charter environments?

RQ3

Does CCI improvement mediate the relationship between TIA implementation and student achievement?

Design & data

Eight years of Texas administrative data and a modern staggered difference-in-differences design that exploits variation in when districts received TIA approval.

56,939Campus-year observations, 2017–2024
8,725Unique schools
1,177Districts
12.44%Of observations treated via district TIA approval

📊 Data sources

  • TAPR — organizational capacity indicators and campus-level achievement (math & reading)
  • AskTED — locations, regions, urbanicity, and charter status for contextual analysis
  • TIA application records — district application timing and approval decisions, obtained via Public Information Request (PIR #67981)

🧭 Identification strategy

  • Staggered DiD — did_multiplegt_dyn estimator (de Chaisemartin et al.), robust to heterogeneous treatment effects
  • Staggered rollout — first approvals in 2020 (15 districts), waves through 2023–24 (112 and 132 districts)
  • Not-yet-treated controls — later adopters serve as controls for earlier ones
  • Controls — standardized economic disadvantage, lagged scores, year FE; SEs clustered by district
  • Pre-trends validated — placebo tests and parallel-trends checks across all specifications

The outcome: the Campus Capacity Index

The CCI is a validated 0–100 composite of school organizational capacity built from six administrative indicators across three dimensions — Experience concentration, Credential quality, and Organizational stability. Optimal Capacity schools outperform Minimal Capacity schools by 4.67pp in math and 3.39pp in reading (district + year FE). Full construction and validation details are in the Paper 1 deep dive.

Finding 1 — Capacity builds over years, not months

TIA produces capacity development that emerges gradually and varies sharply across organizational dimensions. The policy is an Experience-building intervention above all.

Why Year 3 peaks

The trajectory tracks TIA's implementation structure: districts build evaluation systems (Year 1), collect data and make designations (Year 2), and distribute compensation (Year 3) — when incentives activate, effects reach 0.20 SD and statistical significance.

Experience: cumulative advantage

The steadily strengthening Experience effects suggest districts build reputations as desirable workplaces and compete more effectively for experienced teachers — cumulative gains, not one-time recruitment.

Credentials: a redefinition?

Null credential effects may signal a shift toward performance-based rather than credential-based hiring — TIA's rules-in-use redefining teacher quality around demonstrated effectiveness rather than formal qualifications.

Finding 2 — Institutional context moderates everything

TIA effectiveness depends on where it lands. Governance structures prove especially decisive.

Average total effects on overall CCI by baseline context (SD units).

The charter paradox: dimensional effects within charter schools (average total effects, SD units).

Governance
Traditional vs. charter

Traditional public schools show systematic positive effects (+0.14 SD, rising to 0.26–0.30 SD in Years 3–4). Charters go the other way (−0.12 SD; Year 4 at −0.47 SD), driven almost entirely by credentials (−0.50 SD average) — yet still use TIA to improve experience (+0.10) and stability (+0.11). Embedded district evaluation systems appear to enable implementation in ways charter autonomy cannot replicate.

Poverty
High-poverty schools

High-poverty schools average stronger capacity effects (0.11 vs. 0.05 SD), with significant systematic responses on Experience (0.07 SD) and Stability (0.08 SD) — evidence TIA's capacity-building approach addresses resource constraints more effectively than traditional merit pay.

Geography
Rural schools

No significant overall differences — but rural schools show significant dimensional gains on Experience (Year 4 reaching 0.45 SD) and Credentials, highlighting TIA's value for establishing professional staff in hard-to-staff contexts.

Finding 3 — The pathway runs through capacity

A three-step mediation framework (Imai et al.), adapted to the staggered DiD design, separates TIA's direct and indirect pathways to student achievement.

Step 1 · X → M
TIA → capacity

TIA approval builds CCI scores: 0.09 SD average, marginally significant in the four-year window (p = 0.11) but strengthening to p = 0.033 over five years.

Step 2 · M → Y
Capacity → achievement

Schools with CCI improvements gain systematically in math: 0.21 SD average, joint p = 0.01. Reading is positive but weaker (0.17 SD, p = 0.35) — math is more sensitive to organizational coherence.

Step 3 · X → Y | M
Direct effects: minimal

Controlling for capacity, direct TIA effects on achievement are small (0.05 SD math, 0.03 SD reading), not systematic, and fade as implementation matures.

Average total effects per pathway (SD units). Sequential estimation — evidence about mechanisms, not definitive causal mediation.

What this means

Systematic policy-to-capacity effects, strong capacity-to-achievement relationships in mathematics, and minimal direct policy effects: the pattern is consistent with organizational development as the primary mechanism through which performance-based compensation influences outcomes — not the direct incentive-to-performance pathway that traditional agency theory predicts.

Robustness

A comprehensive battery confirms the findings are not artifacts of modeling choices, treatment timing, or the pandemic.

Balance
Pre-treatment balance

Treated and control districts do not differ on baseline CCI (p = 0.42) or math achievement (p = 0.97). Treated districts are larger and higher-poverty — time-invariant differences the design absorbs. No differential missingness by treatment status (all p > 0.09).

Windows
Effects strengthen with time

Five-year specifications reach significance on the overall CCI (joint p = 0.033 vs. 0.108 at four years), with a Year 5 effect of 0.14 SD suggesting sustained development. Experience is robust across all windows (all joint p < 0.003).

Cohorts
Same-switcher restriction

Limiting comparisons to districts treated at similar times, the Experience effect remains highly significant (p < 0.001) and strengthens — 0.14 SD vs. 0.06 SD in the main specification.

Placebo
Placebo & COVID tests

With randomly assigned treatment, only 1 of 20 tests reaches 5% significance — the expected Type I rate. Effects hold across COVID-era and post-pandemic periods, and adding COVID controls leaves estimates unchanged (0.09 SD, p = 0.108).

Implications

What the evidence means for policy design — and where the study's boundaries lie.

Policy 1
Commit for multiple years

Peak effects arrive in Years 3–4. Accountability frameworks expecting immediate achievement gains are misaligned with organizational development timelines.

Policy 2
Differentiate by governance

Traditional public and charter contexts respond in opposite directions. Implementation strategies must account for the institutional systems a school can draw on.

Policy 3
Target strategic recruitment

TIA works through teacher experience deployment. Prioritize strategic recruitment and retention over attempting comprehensive organizational transformation.

Limitations

Findings may not generalize beyond Texas's accountability systems and labor markets. The CCI captures observable administrative characteristics — professional learning communities and collaborative culture remain unmeasured. The mediation analysis relies on sequential estimation rather than definitive causal identification, uses the composite CCI rather than dimension-specific pathways, and the binary improvement indicator sacrifices dose-response detail. The study covers participating districts only, not spillovers.

Conclusion

TIA achieves its effects primarily through organizational capacity development rather than direct individual incentives — challenging prevailing assumptions about performance-based compensation. Sustainable educational improvement requires patient investment in institutional infrastructure rather than simple financial inducements. The TIA model offers a promising alternative to traditional merit pay, but realizing its potential requires reconceptualizing performance-based compensation as organizational development: multi-year commitment, context-specific implementation.
