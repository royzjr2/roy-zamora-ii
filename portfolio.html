Dissertation Research

My three-paper dissertation explores the development, validation, and application of the Campus Capacity Index (CCI). My research demonstrates how organizational capacity serves as a powerful equity mechanism and examines how performance-based policies influence these organizational structures.

The Campus Capacity Index: A Novel Approach to Measuring School Organizational Capacity

Dissertation

This comprehensive dissertation develops and validates the Campus Capacity Index (CCI), analyzing over 56,000 campus observations across 8,605 Texas schools over 7 years. The research establishes a three-factor structure (Experience, Credentials, Stability) that explains up to 61.5% of variance in student outcomes. The work demonstrates that organizational capacity particularly benefits marginalized populations, with emergent bilingual students showing the largest gains (6.27pp in math, 5.96pp in reading). The research also reveals how the Texas Teacher Incentive Allotment influences organizational capacity development over time, with effects peaking in year 3 of implementation.

Key Contributions
  • Novel validated measure using readily available administrative data
  • Clean 3-factor structure explaining 61.5% of total variance
  • Demonstrates organizational capacity as an equity mechanism
  • Evidence of policy effects through organizational pathways
  • Scalable methodology for state and district implementation

Policy Analysis & Design

Research focused on designing and evaluating educational policies, from teacher compensation reform to accountability systems and strategic planning frameworks.

A Partnership to Optimize Educational Outcomes: The Teacher Career Pathway

Policy Design

Comprehensive proposal for a Research Practice Partnership developing a Teacher Career Pathway (TCP) system in a North Texas school district. This five-tier framework addresses teacher empowerment, compensation reform, and professional growth through bottom-up institutional change, creating a sustainable model where teachers drive school improvement as empowered change agents.

Framework Components
  • Five-tier career progression with clear advancement criteria
  • Compensation reaching $100k for master teachers
  • Teacher-led governance and evaluation systems
  • 5-year phased implementation with stakeholder engagement
  • Integration with state funding mechanisms (TIA)

A Strategic Plan for Teacher Compensation Reform in Irving ISD

Economic Analysis

Economic analysis proposing a hybrid compensation model addressing limitations of both efficiency-wage structures and bonus-payment systems. Drawing on economic theory and military compensation frameworks, designs a rank-based pay scale balancing performance incentives with job stability, addressing the problem of imperfect signaling in education while maintaining fiscal sustainability.

Model Features
  • Hybrid efficiency-wage/bonus structure
  • Military-inspired rank progression system
  • $3,500 average salary increase across all teachers
  • $6.5 million implementation cost
  • Addresses experience-earnings gap while rewarding excellence

Evaluation of the Evaluators: Holding District Leadership Accountable

Policy Brief

Policy analysis examining the absence of adequate evaluation systems for district executive leadership in Texas education. Traces the shift from democratic to excellence reforms, revealing how executive leaders have avoided outcomes-based accountability while campus staff face increasing scrutiny, creating power imbalances and inefficiencies in achieving student performance goals.

Policy Recommendations
  • Dynamic evaluation system for executive leadership
  • Strategic planning for accountability culture
  • Measurable objective-setting workshops
  • Infrastructure capacity assessment
  • Alignment with state compliance requirements

Empirical Studies & Research Methods

Quantitative and qualitative research employing diverse methodological approaches to understand educational phenomena and evaluate policy interventions.

Exploring the Impact of School Choice on Academic Achievement and Attendance

Quasi-Experimental

Instrumental variable analysis examining causal effects of attending secondary schools of choice on student achievement and attendance. Using lottery-based assignment as an instrument with 2,441 students, finds heterogeneous treatment effects: choice schools benefit already-proficient students but show negative effects for struggling learners, raising critical equity questions.

Methodological Approach
  • Instrumental variable regression with lottery instrument
  • 2,441 student cohort analysis
  • Heterogeneous treatment effects by prior achievement
  • Controls for demographics and parent education
  • Separate analysis for gifted students

Will Teacher Experience Be Enough to Remediate COVID Learning Loss

Statistical Analysis

Empirical investigation of whether student exposure to experienced teachers can remediate COVID-19 learning losses in middle school reading. Analysis of 1,766 students reveals complex heterogeneous effects: while experienced teachers benefit proficient students (58.23 point scale score increase), the effect reverses for struggling learners, with particularly concerning implications for equity.

Key Findings
  • 174.69 point effect for highest experience tier (18% score increase)
  • Negative effects (-46.88 points) for below-grade students
  • Economically disadvantaged Black/Latinx students cannot reach proficiency
  • Teacher experience alone insufficient for equity
  • Need for targeted interventions beyond experience

Key Arguments
